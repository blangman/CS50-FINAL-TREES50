Lobed Leaves

One common type of leaf is a lobed leaf, which is very common among the Oak family specifically, within Harvard Yard, there are 6 different species of trees that have lobed leaves, all of which come from the Oak Family, which has a scientific name of Quercus. These are the trees in the yard that produce all of the acorns that fall on people's heads during the fall!!


Red Oak (Quercus Rubra)

The Leaf of the Red Oak has pointed lobes. The lobes of the leaf refers to the different compartments of the leaf that appear to be protruding out. In the Red Oak, these lobes are pointed. Normally, there are between 7 to 11 lobes on each leaf of a Red Oak, and these leaves can vary in size, with large leaves growing up to 9 inches long!

The bark of the Red Oak is quite unique. Red Oak bark is usually (unsurprisingly) red in color but has lines of silver that run through the bark. As the tree ages, fisures form which appear as "cracks" in the tree. These cracks contribute to the scaly appearance of the Red Oak.

Red Oak trees have multiple ways of reproducing. A Red Oak tree is actually able to reproduce in two different ways: through seeds and through it's roots. Reproduction through seeds is the most common form of reproduction among plants, and little has to be explained about it. But, how Red Oaks can also reproduce is by roots which is essentially when a new shoot of the tree starts growing up from the roots.

White Oak (Quercus Alba)

The Leaf of the White Oak has curved lobes. Although many people confuse Red Oaks with White Oaks, the key difference comes with their leaf shapes! The Red Oak has leaves that are pointed at their lobes, while the white oak has no points and is entirely curvey. Each leaf usually contains 7 to 9 lobes, and each leaf can grow to be about 5 inches long.

The bark of a White Oak is very intriguing. White Oak bark, unlike Red Oaks which just has the appearance of it, is actually broken into scales. This is due to the fact that as the tree ages, it grows furrows, which then break into scales. Oftentimes, when you look closey at a White Oak, you can find patches of bark that are entirely smooth, and that is where recent scales have fallen off of the bark to make way for the new wood underneath.

White Oak Bark actually serves many purposes. For centuries, people have been using the bark of White Oaks for its medicinal qualities. White Oak bark can be made into a tea that is used to treat various illnesses such as arthritis, diarrhea, and fevers. Other cultures have used White Oak bark in a compress and rubbed it onto areas of swelling in an attempt to stop inflammation. Unlike the Red Oak which gets its name from its bark, the White Oak gets its name because during the summer and spring seasons, the underside of the leaves appears to be a white-ish green color.

Swamp White Oak (Quercus Bicolor)

Similar to the White Oak, the Leaf of the Swamp White Oak has curved lobes. The Swamp White Oak, as it's name would suggest, has a very similar leaf shape to the White Oak, with both the White and Swamp White Oaks having rounded lobes. Swamp White Oak leaves have more lobes than do White Oak containing between 9 and 11 lobes usually. Swamp White Oak tree leaves grow to be between 5 and 7 inches long.

The bark of the Swamp White Oak is similar to that of the White Oak. The bark of the Swamp White Oak has a similar scaly appearance as that of the White Oak. When the tree is young, the bark is exceptionally flimsy and often times will look as if it is peeling off the tree. As the tree ages, the bark grows deeper furrows and instead gains a scaly effect.

Swamp White Oak was named for a cool feature of it's leaves. The scientific name of the Swamp White Oak is quercus bicolor, with bicolor meaning "two colors". The two colors that the name refers to is the dark green color of the top of the leaf and the bright white color of the bottoms of the leaves. The common name for the tree, Swamp White Oak, was named after the tree's preference for swampy and humid environments.

Scarlet Oak (Quercus Coccinea)

The Scarlet Oak has similar to the Red Oak, with some key differences. Unlike the previous two trees, the lobes on the Scarlet Oak are pointed. The Scarlet Oak leaves mostly all have 7 lobes, with very few containing 9. These leaves can grow between 4 and 7 inches. To tell Scarlet Oak and Red Oak leaves apart, you can look at the spaces between the lobes as Scarlet Oaks have a much more drastic indent between lobes as opposed to the Red Oak.

Similar to the rest of the Oak family, the Scarlet Oak has scaly bark. Similar to the rest of the oak family, the Scarlet Oak develops furrows as it ages. But, unlike other species in the family, these furrows are not very deep and instead remain rather close to the surface. The inner bark between the furrows is a pink color when the tree is young, and as the tree ages this inner bark turns orange.

The Scarlet Oak is a very resilient tree. The Scarlet Oak has adapted to grow in dry environments. The tree can grow along ridges and slopes with an elaborate root system. Because of this extensive root system, it means several things for the tree: For one, the tree is much harder to uproot because of it's ability to grow along ridges. For another thing, the Scarlet Oak is able to survive droughts much better than other trees because its elaborate trunk allows for optimal water storage.

Pin Oak (Quercus Palustris)

The Pin Oak has the most simplified lobed leaves of the oaks in Harvard Yard. The leaves of the Pin Oak are very similar to those of the Scarlet Oak, only they are simpler. Pin Oak leaves mostly have 5 lobes, all of which are pointed. Also similar to the Scarlet Oak, the gaps between lobes in the Pin Oak are very drastic, meaning that the biggest distinction comes with the amount of lobes on the leaf.

Unlike most of the oaks, the Pin Oak has soft bark. When the Pin Oak is young, the bark is extremely soft and smooth. As the tree grows, the bark begins to grow furrows, but unlike oaks like the Red Oak or White Oak, the Pin Oak never gets deep furrows, only growing deep enough to make small lines in the bark, but the bark itself stays rather smooth.

The Pin Oak is a tree of many names. The name Pin Oak derives from the fact that when Pin Oak branches are young, they are extremely sharp and durable, which is the reverse of most trees, which start their branches off as weak, soft wood. But, while this is the common name, the Pin Oak is also known as the Swamp Oak (Not to be confused with White Swamp Oak), the Water Oak, or the Spanish Oak for its ability to grow in wet and swampy environments.

Black Oak (Quercus Velutina)

The Black Oak leaf is similar to that of other oaks, but with one special characteristic. Upon first glance, the leaves of the Black Oak look strikingly similar to those of the Scarlet Oak or even the Red Oak. Black Oak leaves have 7 to 9 lobes, and the space between the lobes can be either shallow like the Red Oak or deep like the Scarlet Oak, so that is not useful to tell them apart. What you can use to tell these leaves apart is the fact that at the tip of each lobe on the Black Oak, there is a sharp bristle which is often yellowish, which no other oaks have.

The bark of the Black Oak is where it gets its name. Unlike the White Oak and Swamp White Oak, which get their names from their leaves, the Black Oak is named for it's dark grey to black bark. Similar to a lot of the oak family, the bark of the Black Oak is furrowed and has a scaly appearance. The inner bark between the furrows also has a dark coloration, being a deep orange color.

The scientific names derives from it's bud's fuzzy nature. When you think about Black Oaks, I bet you don't think of them as being fuzzy, but actually their scientific name, Quercus Velutina, is derived from the Latin word for "fleece"! This is because the buds of the tree, which grow into acorns, are exceptionally fuzzy when they begin their growth process in the spring!
