Miscellaneous Leaves

There are some leaves that don't fall into any of the five previous categories but are still important to Harvard Yard! Here we will explore 5 different tree species that have miscellaneously shaped leaves in Harvard Yard!


Ginkgo (Ginkgo Biloba)

The leaves of the Ginkgo Tree are a unique fan shape which have been around for millions of years! The leaves of the ginkgo tree are extremely distinct from other leaves, having a wonderful fan shape to them. In addition to their speical shape, ginkgo leaves have an amazing history behind them. The leaves of ginkgo trees were found fossilized from as far back as 200 million years ago! That's back when dinosaurs walked the earth and this tree has remain largely unchanged since then! These leaves are also famous for the bright yellow color that they turn in the fall, which lights up the entire tree.

Ginkgo bark changes color and texture as the tree matures. A young Ginkgo tree has gray bark which is rather smooth. As the tree ages, not only does the bark become more deeply furrowed, but the color also changes. Older Ginkgo trees have a browner bark color than their younger counterparts. The wood of Ginkgos is extremely weak, which makes it an almost worthless tree commercially.

Ginkgo fruit is extremely foul smelling for a good evolutionary reason! Female Ginkgo Trees produce berries, which are how the tree reproduces. But, once these berries fall to the ground they start emitting a foul smell, which can be described as smelling like rotting flesh! The reason why these berries release this smell is because it attracts carnivorous animals to eat the berries and then once the berries are consumed, the seeds will be pooped out by the animals in another place and thus allows for the tree to reproduce! And, since this tree has been around since the time of dinosaurs, you can imagine that some of those predators that have been attracted to the smell of ginkgo berries were dinosaurs themselves!

European Beech (Fagus Sylvatica)

European Beech leaves have hair on them! Yes, you read that correctly, trees can have hair! If you look closely on the edge of the European Beech, you can see small hair follicles surrounding the entire leaf. These little hairs serve as a defense mechanism for the tree to fend off against predators. For small insects, these little hairs serve essentially as barbs that have the ability to get them stuck to the leaf and therefore be vulnerable to predators, and for larger predators, these hairs are annoying and irritating in their mouth, which serves as a reminder to not eat that leaf anymore.

European Beech bark is known for it's extreme smoothness. European Beech Trees are extremely famous because of their smooth and delicate bark. The bark is very thin, which means many things for the tree. For one, European Beeches are extremely popular among young lovers because it is the perfect tree to carve your name on in a heart, which is done a lot. But, it also the thinness and delicacy of the bark is harmful because it can lead to the bark being damaged more easily than most species of trees.

Why the European Beech is not in the Teardrop category is because these leaves are not always teardrop shaped! Despite what the image in the Leaf section shows, European Beech leaves do not always have a Teardrop shape. They begin the spring season crumpled in almost a ball, and spend the next few months since Spring unfurling into their full teardrop shape.

Common Hawthorn (Crataegus Monogyna)

The Common Hawthorn leaf is a mixture of several leaf types which we have talked about. The Common Hawthorn leaf could be categorized as both a lobed leaf and also as a pointed leaf. The overall shape of the leaf is very similar to most of the Maples that we have seen, yet the leaf does not have many points that deine it. Instead of points, each segment of the leaf is curved rather than pointed, which puts this leaf in the miscellaneous categories.

The bark of the Common Hawthorn is extremely flaky. The bark of this tree is a grey to brown color and develops great fissures in it as the tree ages. Another interesting thing about the bark and trunk of the tree is that this tree is known for growing rather haphazardly, with the tree shape becoming very knotted, and even occasionally the tree will loop back in on itself and a branch will reattach at a later location!

The Hawthorn is known in it's local environment for it's beautiful berries! Another name for the Common Hawthorn is the English Hawthorn because it is native to England. But, within England, it has a different name; it is known as the May-Tree. This is because the Hawthorn has brilliant bright red berries which appear in full bloom in May.

Little-Leaf Linden (Tilia Cordata)

Little Leaf Linden leaves have a heart shape! The leaves of the Little Leaf Linden are in the miscellaneous category because they have a unique heart shape to them. From the stem, the leaf curves back towards the brach before returning to create a point, which forms a heart! Some leaves on the Linden don't develop properly and therefore only one side does the dip inward like a heart.

The Little Leaf Linden bark has shallow ridged that form into plates. The bark of the Little Leaf Linden is a gray color with furrows that don't go too deep into the bark. The wood underneath the bark is extremely fibrous. Unlike a lot of scaly trees which begin their lives as smooth, the Little Leaf Linden is scaly for almost all of it's life.

Little Leaf Linden Wood is not very hardy, but it does have many sculptural qualities! Most types of wood that are valuable in trades are valuable for their durability and strength, but the Little Leaf Linden is valuable for the exact opposite reason! Because of the wood's pliability and ability to be easily shapen, it is popular in both wood sculpting and also used to create musical instruments such as recorders and the bodies of guitars!

Redbud (Cercis Canadensis)

Similar to the Little Leaf Linden, Redbuds have very distinct heart-shaped leaves. The leaves of the Redbud can grow to be up to five inches long and are even more of a distinctive heart shape than that of the Little Leaf Linden. Whereas the Linden tends to have leaves that are only heart shaped on one half, the Redbud has heart-shaped leaves throughout the entire tree.

The Redbud Tree has a flaky bark, which unveils a deep orange or even maroon layer of wood underneath. The bark of the Redbud Tree is a dark brown to black color, and as the tree grows it becomes more flaky, with pieces of bark falling off rather easily. When the bark falls off, it exposes the underlayer of wood which has a very interesting dark orange to maroon color, making this tree's wood very unique and special.

The Redbud is so resplendent and beautifully adorned with flowers that it caught to attention of a certain important historical figure. The Redbud is a tree that is relatively easy to maintain, and as such it can be found throughout almost all of the United States, which has been the case for hundreds of years. One day, while he was hiking in the woods in Pennsylvania, George Washington spotted a redbud and loved it so much that he order that redbuds be uprooted from the Pennsylvania forest and brought to his house in Mount Vernon!
