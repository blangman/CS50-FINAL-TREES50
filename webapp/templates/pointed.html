Pointed Leaves

Another very common type of leaf in Harvard Yard is pointed leaves! These leaves are distinguishable by their star-like shapes and sharp edges. Pointed leaves are one of the biggest categories of leaves in the yard, with 9 species of Yard trees having pointed leaves.


Sweetgum (Liquidambar Styraciflua)

The leaves of the Sweetgum Tree are a distinct five-pointed star shape. More than most leaves, Sweetgum Tree leaves have a very accurate star shape. Depending on in what soil the Sweetgum is grown in, the leaves of the tree can either turn yellow or red in the fall. The Sweetgum that grows in Harvard Yard turns yellow in the fall!

The Sweetgum grows deeper ridges with age. When Sweetgum trees are young, their barks are very smooth and they remain that way usually for the first 25 years of their life. At around the 25 year mark, the tree begins to develop ridges, which only deeper as the tree ages. Right underneath the bark there is a rich golden sap, which is where the tree gets it's name.

Sweetgum Trees have extremely unique and interesting looking fruits! The fruits of the Sweetgum Tree are extremely interesting looking fruits. The fruit is a green sphere covered in spikes, which looks not unsimilar to a microscopic image of a virus! These fruits only start developing on the tree once it ages to be between 20 to 30 years.

Red Maple (Acer Rubrum)

The Red Maple leaf is one of the most iconic leaves in North America. The Red Maple leaf is a very popular symbol, especially if you have ever seen the map of Canada. Another example of this leaf being popular is the NHL team the Toronto Maple Leaves. This leaf been the symbol of Canada since the 1800s. This leaf has three primary points with two smaller points on the bottom of the leaf. Within each point of the leaf, there are several points. The leaf became such a popular symbol because of the Red Maple's many uses from timber to syrup and many uses in between.

This tree grows furrows in a different way than most trees that we've seen so far. Up until now, most of the trees that have scaly bark have furrows that cause individual "scales" on the tree. Instead, the Red Maple has really long fissures that instead of forming "scales" form lines down the trunk of the tree.

Although it is called the "Red" Maple, it also can turn yellow or orange in the fall! The color that the Red Maple turns is depending on the soil that it is planted in! With healthy soil that is nitrogen rich, the tree turns it's signature red color. But, if the tree is planted in less then optimal conditions, the tree's foliage can instead turn orange or yellow.

American Sycamore (Platanus Occidentalis)

The American Sycamore leaf is a large and simple leaf that has between 3 and 5 main points. The leaves of the American Sycamore grow to be between 4 to 8 inches long, and all of the veins on a sycamore leaf stem from the same one point, which isn't always true for most leaves.

The American Sycamore has a flaky and smooth bark that looks almost painted on. As the American Sycamore ages, layers of bark fall off of the tree. But, unlike a lot of other trees that lose layers of bark, the American Sycamore does so in a smooth fashion, leaving no splinters behind. When the bark peels away you can see how each layer of bark after the surface layer becomes lighter and lighter until eventually the wood is white.

The name "Sycamore" actually comes from a misindentification of the tree! The name Sycamore derives from the Greek word "Sukomoras" which means "fig-mulberry". This was then adopted into the english "Sycamore", even though they are two different trees!

Hedge Maple (Acer Campestre)

The Hedge Maple leaf retains the iconic Maple leaf shape, but with rounded corners. The shape of the Maple Leaf is iconic, but not all maples have the same leaf shape. The tips of the of the Hedge Maple leaves are rounded, making the tree distinguishable from the famous symbol while still being a clear member of the family.

The Hedge Maple has scaly bark that often grows fungi. For some unknown reason, scientists have found that the bark of the Hedge Maple is exceptionally susceptible to growing fungi and moss. Similar to a lot of trees that we have seen, the tree bark starts off rather smooth when the tree is young and then as it grows the bark cracks and harders into a scaly texture.

Hedge Maples are very well-used in gardenscaping. The word "Hedge" in Hedge Maple is there because the tree is very durable when being pruned, which allows the tree, when grown next to other Hedge Maples, to be created into artistic hedges. Other trees when they are pruned suffer greatly and don't have strong enough defenses to be able to regrow the parts that they lost, but the Hedge Maple is remarkable at having durability and growing back.

Norway Maple (Acer Platanoides)

The Norway Maple leaf is distinguishable from the Red Maple for the opposite reason as the Hedge Maple! If the Red Maple is the symbol of maple leaves, and Hedge Maples are differentiable because of their rounded points, then the Norway Maple has the opposite distinction! The tips of Norway Maple points have small, often white barbs that are sharp to the touch!

The bark of the Norway Maple is a gray color with black tints. The bark of the Norway Maple is a very furrowed bark that makes a scaly texture. The bark of the Norway Maple is really thick, which makes the tree rather good at resisting wild fires. The bark on the branches is much smooth than the bark on the trunk, with the branch barking have a mostly smooth texture.

The Norway Maple is a very important tree in Europe, where it has many uses. Since the Norway Maple is native to Europe, the European people have been using its resources for many uses. The wood of the Norway Maple is very hardy, which makes it resistant to fires but also makes it into a wonderful woof for crafting long-lasting furniture and flooring. Actually, the first Stradivarious Violins were made from the wood of Norway Maples.

Sugar Maple (Acer Saccharum)

The Sugar Maple leaves are similar to that of the Norway Maple except the tips of the points are not sharp. The similarities between the Sugar Maple Leaf and the Norway Maple leaf are abundant, with both of them being distinguishable from the Red Maple Leaf because they are more pointed, but the difference between the Sugar Maple and the Norway Maple are that the Norway Maple tips end in sharp points whereas in the Sugar Maple, the tips are still the same leaf material.

The Bark of the Sugar Maple consists of flaky scales. One thing about the bark of the Sugar Maple is that it is extremely thick, even giving the Sugar Maple the name Hard Maple in other parts of the world. Because the bark is so thick, it allows for the sap inside to grow undisturbed from animals and the outside environment. For that reason, the sap is extracted and used as Maple Syrup, which is widely popular in North America!

A lot of Sugar Maple sap goes in to producing one gallon of Maple Syrup! When the Sugar Maple produces it's syrup, that syrup is made primarily of water, being the tree's main source of sustenance. When the Maple Syrup is then removed from the tree, the water has to be boiled out so that the syrup is concentrated enough to be the right consistancy and flavor. That means that you need to extract around 40 gallons of sap from Sugar Maples in order to make 1 gallon of Maple Syrup. Since each tree produces between 10 to 20 gallons of sap a year, that means that each gallon container of Maple Syrup involves several trees.

Japanese Maple (Acer Palmatum)

The leaf of the Japanese Maple is quite distinguishable from other Maples! Unlike most species within the Maple family, the Japanese Maple's points stem from rather close to the base of the leaf. This results in more defined points on the leaf and a stronger resemblance to a star shape. Within each point of the Japanese Maple leaf, there are ridges, making the shape of the leaf even more unique from most other maples. This is mostly due in part to the evolutionary separation between Japanese Maples and the rest of the family.

The bark of the Japanese Maple tree is extremely unique, being a coral red color. Unique to the Japanese Maple tree is it's stunning bark, which often gives the plant the name "Coral Maple". This bark remains very smooth throughout the entire life of the Japanese Maple and is yet another example of how different evolutionarily the Japanese Maple is from the other maples that grow in Harvard Yard from Europe and North America.

The Japanese Maple is most commonly a dwarf tree! Most Maple trees that exist on campus are towering trees that grow very rapidly, but the Japanese Maple Tree is the exact opposite. Back in the 1800s, the Japanese Maple was a common tree across Japan that grew to impressive heights and dominated forests, but in the late 1800s, during Japan's industrial revolution, this tree was over lumbered. The only trees that remained of the Japanese Maples were ones that farmers and individuals had been growing in their homes and yards, which were primarily dwarf trees. This means that now in the modern day, the majority of all Japanese Maples are dwarf trees, being ancestors of the small garden trees that survived the industrial revolution.

Tuliptree (Liriodendron Tulipifera)

Tuliptrees have unique four-pointed leaves! Up until now in the Pointed Leaves category, we have only seen leabes that are either 5 pointed or 3 pointed, but the tuliptree is unique in that it has 4 pointed leaves! This makes it easily distinguishable from other pointed-leaf trees. The leaves of the tuliptree also can grow rather large, sometimes growing to be 6 to 8 inches long!

The bark of the Tuliptree has very interesting undertones! The bark of the Tuliptree is mostly gray with green undertones in it. Often, there are white spots on the trunk of the tree which are a natural occurence because of the wood of the bark. The bark texture is scaly, with a diamond pattern forming from the furrows in the bark.

The Tuliptree doesn't grow flowers for the first fews years of it's life! The Tuliptree, to no one's surprise, is most known for the beautiful tulip flowers that grow from the branches! But, these gorgeous and colorful flowers don't start growing on the tree until the tree reaches 15 to 20 years old! But, worry not because those 15-20 years is nothing compared to the lifespan of the tree! Tuliptrees can live to be upwards of 350 years old!

Common Holly (Ilex Aquifolium)

The Holly leaf is an iconic symbol of the holidays in the Western World! Holly leaves are extremely distinctive, garnering a deep, waxy shade of green and very many points. The holly leaf, and subsequently berry, has become a staple of the Holiday Season because, unlike some berries, Holly berries remain on the tree all winter, which leads to a remarkable contrast between the berries and the snow. The Holly leaves themselves are spiky to discourage potential predators like birds from standing on them and eating away at the berries.

The bark of the Common Holly is an extremely smooth bark! We have seen many kinds of trees which are smooth as youth and then as they mature they develop furrows. The Holly Tree is not one of these trees. The Holly Tree remains smooth throughout it's lifetime, only ever becoming fractured if the tree contracts a disease or covers up a damaged spot.

The Holly leaves remain on the tree year-round! In the Needles tab of Tree S 50 you can learn that not all needle-bearing trees are evergreen and keep their needles year-round, some of them are deciduous! Well, the Holly is the opposite of that, a leaf-bearing tree that DOES keep it's leaves yearround! The Holly is a rare example of an evergreen tree with full leaves!
