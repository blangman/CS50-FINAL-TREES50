String of Leaflets

There are some trees in Harvard Yard where one individual leaf looks like a string of smaller leaves. These are known as Compound Leaves, and yes, they are only ONE leaf. This category is a fairly broad reaching category, containing 9 different species of trees in the yard.


Honey Locust (Gleditsia Triacanthos)

The leaf of a honeylocust contains many teardrop-shaped leaflets! The leaf of a Honey Locust often consists of over 20 small leaflets, each leaflet growing to between .75 inches to 1 inch. The Honey Locust leaflets are perfectly symmetrical, meaning that when there is a leaflet growing on one side of a leaf, one of identical size will be growing on the other side. This can also be extended to each branch of the tree, with leaves also sharing that symmetry.

The Honey Locust has an extremely interesting defense mechanism on it's bark! The bark of the Honey Locust is a red-brown color and it has a scaly texture to it. But also, as the tree matures, the bark begins to develop large sharp thorns, which help the tree ward off predators like woodpeckers! These spikes are also doubly effective because if you are in contact with one of them, the spike releases spores which can cause a skin reaction in most animals. This rash takes a long time to recover from in animals, which acts as a long-term reminder to avoid the tree.

Honey Locust Trees don't actually produce any honey!!! This statement alone is pretty obvious, we all know that bees are the ones who produce honey. But how then did the Honey Locust get it's name? It actually got it's name because of the taste of the seeds. Honey Locust seeds were very commonly eaten among Native Americans and are said to have a very sweet taste! In many Native American cultures, Honey Locust seeds would be used in a similar way to coffee beans today to create a drink similar to coffee!

Japanese Pagoda (Styphnolobium Japonicum)

Unlike the compound leaves of the Honey Locust, the Japanese Pagoda Tree has an odd number of leaflets per leaf. One of the defining characteristics of the Japanese Pagoda Leaves is that all of the leaflets are symmetrical until the end of the leaf, where the tip is defined by one larger leaflet that juts out. Another way to tell these two apart is that the Honey Locust has leaflets that get smaller as it approaches the end of the leaf, whereas the Japanese Pagoda leaflets actually get larger the closer they are to the end of the leaf.

The bark of the Japanese Pagoda is organized in long-lined furrows that separate the tree into vertical lines. These furrows develop in the tree with age, and are a defining part of the tree's scaly bark texture. The scales of the tree are also known to flake off, especially if it is planted in poor soil conditions. When the tree doesn't have proper soil conditions, the bark becomes weak and then flakes off very easily.

The Japanese Pagoda Tree is not actually from Japan!!! Despite what the name suggests, the Japanese Pagoda tree is not originally from Japan, but rather is from China! This explains why it has one of it's other names: The Chinese Scholar Tree! Why the tree is credited as being from Japan is because the tree was popularized as a tree that was very commonly planted around Japanese Buddhist temples. This is also how it gets the name "Pagoda", with a Pagoda being the name of a Buddhist temple.

Golden Rain Tree (Koelreuteria Paniculata)

The Golden Rain Tree's leaflets are shockingly complex amongst leaflets. The leaves of the Golden Rain Tree are made up up of several rather large leaflets. The larger leaflets can grow to be around 3 to 4 inches. The shape of the overall leaf is that one that swells in the middle, with the leaves closer to the branch being smaller, then swelling, then once again becoming small at the tip of the leaf.

The Golden Rain Tree has flaky bark that unveils the bright orange wood underneath. The Golden Rain Tree has bark that goes through several changes throughout it's lifetime. In the beginning, like almost all trees, the bark begins smooth. Then, as the tree ages, the bark begins to become scaly, with the furrows showing the bright orange color of the wood underneath. Then, as the tree continues to age, the scaly nature of the tree then becomes flaky and the tree begins to shed it's bark in a flaky way.

The Golden Rain Tree has several features that can be used to make dyes! The Golden Rain Tree has bean pods, and within the bean pods there are dark colored seeds that the tree uses for reproduction. While they are used for reproduction, they can also be used to be ground up and made into black dye, which has been happening for thousands of years in China and India! That is not the only part of the flower which can be made into dye! The bright yellow flowers from which the tree gets it's name can be ground up and made into a yellow dye!

Black Locust (Robinia Pseudoacacia)

The Black Locust leaf is very similar to the Honey Locust leaf, but with two main exceptions! For one, the shape of the Black Locust and Honey Locust are slightly different, with the Honey Locust have more pointed leaflets than the Black Locust. For another thing, the Black Locust has an odd number of leaflets while the Honey Locust has an even number of leaflets. Black Locusts have one singular leaflet at the end of the leaf whereas the Honey Locust ends in a bud.

The Black Locust has a similar defense mechanism as the Honey Locust, but slightly less extreme. We saw earlier that the Honey Locust has the peculiar trait that it has sharp thorns which protect it from predators. The Black Locust has a similar trait, but it is less extreme for the Black Locust. Black Locust bark grows small thorns on it, which are used in the same manner. The thorns don't need to be as large as the Honey Locust because the bark of and seeds of the tree are already poisonous for animals!

Black Locusts are helpful in both your lawn and to regrow forests after fire! Black Locust wood is extremely durable, hard, and resistant to rot. This makes it the perfect wood to use for fence posts in people's lawns! The resistant bark is also useful when it comes to repopulating a forest after fire because the wood is able to survive all the environmental disaster that comes with a fire. These trees are also ideal for repopulation because they reproduce through their roots which makes them able to reproduce really quickly and repopulate a forest in only a couple of years.

Black Ash (Fraxinus Nigra)

The Black Ash leaves are different than most compound leaves! Most of the trees that we have seen thusfar with compound leaves have very many small leaflets that add up to create one leaf. The difference with the Black Ash is that instead of many small leaflets, it's leaves consist of a few large leaflets which all contribute to make one leaf. Each leaflet is a long teardrop shape, with each leaflet growing up to 4 inches long!

The bark of the Black Ash is very flaky. Black Ash bark is extremely flaky throughout it's lifespan but also develops furrows with age. The color of the bark can span from a reddish-brown color to gray, with the ladder being more common.

The shape of the Black Ash changes as it ages! When a Black Ash is young, the tree takes on a pyramidal shape (almost like a christmas tree), but as the tree ages, the taller branches of the tree grow faster than the slower ones which eventually leads to a more circular shape. Also as it ages, the wood of the tree becomes more and more valuable, being able to be used as a darkwood to create furniture.

White Ash (Fraxinus Americana)

The White Ash leaves and Black Ash leaves have several key differences. For one, the White Ash has much more rounded leaflets than does the Black Ash. Both types of ashes adopt the same leaf structure where the leaf gets thicker the closer to the end of the leaf, but the White Ash ends in a leaf that is much bigger than the previous ones while the Black Ash ends in a leaf that is approximately the same size as the ones adjacent to it.

The furrows on the White Ash create a beautiful diamond pattern! As the White Ash ages, the tree does not develop fissures in the same way that a lot of other species do, but instead it develops them almost as engravings. The furrows create a beautiful pattern in the bark of the White Ash that looks like diamonds.

The White Ash wood has many uses. White Ash is also known as "Poor Man's Oak" among the lumber community because it still produces hard and strong wood, but at a much cheaper cost and not as weather resistant as Oak. White Ash wood was what the original Louisville Sluggers were made out of which is an interesting piece of sports history!

Eastern Black Walnut Tree (Juglans Nigra)

The leaves of Black Walnut Trees can grow to be extremely long! The entirety of the leaf of a Black Walnut Tree can grow to be about 2 feet long! These leaves consist of around 20 leaflets, each of which usually grows between 2 to 4 inches long. Black Walnut leaves have an even number of leaflets, being symmetric on both sides of the leaves. As it gets closer to the end of the leaf, the leaflets become less rounded and more pointed.

As the name suggests, the bark of the Black Walnut is black in color! One of the defining characteristics of the Black Walnut tree is the signature black bark. The bark is deeply furrowed, which leads to a scaly texture of the bark. The wood directly beneath the bark is also black, which means that the deep fissures only further emphasize the black coloration.

Walnuts actually release a toxic chemical which inhibits other tree growth. The word "Juglans" in the scientific name for the Black Walnut is derived from the chemical "juglone", which exists within walnuts and serves as a method to prevent competition. When the walnuts fall from the tree, they are more likely to fall nearby the mother plant, and then once they are on the ground they release juglone, which is toxic to many other kinds of trees. This serves as a way to make sure that all of the area near the base of the tree is kept undisturbed so that no other tree could potentially out-compete the Black Walnut tree in that area.

Kentucky Coffee Tree (Gymnocladus Dioicus)

Kentucky Coffee Tree leaflets grow in a different pattern than any leaves we have seen so far! When it comes to leaflets so far, either they have a symmetrical counterpart, or they are at the end of the leaf. Meanwhile, in the Kentucky Coffee Tree, the leaflets are spaced out so that they attach to the center of the leaf in increments, which can be seen in the image. One leaflet will be attached on the right, then slightly further up one is attached on the left and this pattern continues.

The bark of the Kentucky Coffee Tree is flaky and because of this forms layers. The Kentucky Coffee Tree has extremely flaky bark, which means that when the tree is hit with wind or weather, parts of the bark are prone to fall off. What this creates is a sort of collage of bark that has been on the tree for the longest time contrasted with the new bark that exists underneath the pieces that just fell.

Kentucky Coffee Trees do not actually produce coffee at all! This is another classic example of mistaken naming. When settlers came to North America for the firs time, they realized the resemblance that the Kentucky Coffee Tree had to real coffee beans, so they named the tree after that resemblance. The colonists then also tried to use the Coffee Tree beans to make a substitute coffee, which worked but was not particularly good tasting.

Yellowwood (Cladrastis Kentukea)

Yellowwood leaves share traits that we have seen in several other trees all mixed together! The shape of the Yellowwood leaf is similar to that of the White Ash, but the way that the leaflets are lines up is more reminiscent of the way that the Kentucky Cofee Tree grows. Even though the shape is similar to the White Ash, it is not identical as the tips of the Yellowwod leaflets are more pointed than those of the White Ash.

Yellowwood Trees have an exceptional smooth bark their entire lifespan! While most trees that are smooth in their youth grow out of their smooth bark, the Yellowwood remains smooth throughout it's entire life. The bark is a gray to white color, sometimes being a combination of both white and gray in some beautiful patterns!

Although the leaves turn yellow in the fall, that is not where the Yellow in the name comes from! The name "Yellowwood" comes from the fact that when the wood is freshly cut, the core is yellow! This yellow wooden core was used to create yellow dyes by early settlers in North America!
