Needles

Despite what some people think, needles are actually leaves! Trees that have needles produce cones (like pine cones) and are called conifers! While most conifers are evergreen, you will soon learn that some actually do shed their needles for the winter!


Eastern White Pine (Pinus Strobus)

The Needles of the Eastern White Pine are soft, flexible, and grow together. The needles of the Eastern White Pine can grow rather large, growing up to 5 inches long. One distinguishing fact about them is that when you touch them, they are not extremely pointy and stiff but are rather soft and flexible to the touch. Another distinguishing fact about these needles is that wherever you see them sprouting from the main branch, they grow in clusters of 5 needles per bud.

The bark of the Eastern White Pine changes drastically as it grows. When an Eastern White Pine is young, the bark is a grayish-green color and is very smooth. As the tree grows up, the bark turns into a dark grey color and starts to develop furrows and cracks in the surface of the bark.

The Eastern White Pine tree played an important part in American History! The word "white" in the name comes from the fact that when the tree is freshly cut down for lumbering, the wood is a white color. This led to it being heavily sought after in the US colonies in the 18th century. Colonial Americans would cut down the trees and make a profit off of their wood, but the british government didn't like that because they wanted to be the ones to make the profit. This led to the British making a law that all trees that had trunks greater than 2 feet in diameter belonged to the British government. This law applied to all trees, but was specifically passed because of their desire for the Eastern White Pine! This law added to the rising tension between colonists and the british government, and the rest is history!

Swiss Stone Pine (Pinus Cembra)

The Swiss Stone Pine has long needles that are usually a deep green. The needles of the Swiss Stone Pine grow in clumps of 5, similar to the Eastern White Pine. These needles grow to be between 2 to 3 inches long and are also rather soft and flexible to the touch. There are some mutations of the Swiss Stone Pine that grow needles that are a bluish green color, which have become popular as Christmas trees in many places.

The bark of the Swiss Stone Pine becomes very flaky as the tree ages. When the tree is young, the bark is extremely smooth and undisturbed. The tree grows very slowly, so the bark of the tree remains smooth for around the first 50 years of the tree's life. Eventually, when the trunk of the tree has a great enough diameter, the bark starts to create furrows and it becomes extremely flaky, often falling off of the tree with passing storms and wind.

Swiss Stone Pines provide a popular food source to humans. Swiss Stone Pines, along with other members of the pine family, are harvested by humans for their pine nuts. Pine nuts are actually the seeds that are hidden within the cones of pines. Despite their name, pine nuts are not in fact nuts but they are seeds!

Austrian Pine (Pinus Nigra)

The needles of the Austrian Pine are unique to the other pines in Harvard Yard. The two other species of Pine in Harvard Yard have needles that grow in clumps of 5. The Austrian Pine on the other hand has needles that grow in groups of 2. These needles form rings around the branch that they are growing from, creating a spiraling effect. The needles grow to be between 3 and 6 inches long and also unlike the other pine trees in the Yard, the needles are stiff. Although they are stiff, they are still flexible and do not break when bended.

Austrian Pine bark is one of it's defining features. The bark of the Austrian Pine is a dark grey to black color and has a scaly appearance to it. As the tree grows, deep fissures grow in the tree, with black inner bark between fissures. The bark of the Austrian Pine is also extremely fragile and is prone to flaking off in bad weather conditions like wind or storms. When the bark of the tree flakes off, it unveils a layer of orange wood underneath the bark.

Austrian Pines have multiple names. The scientific name of the Austrian Pine is Pinus Nigra, with Nigra deriving from the Latin word for black. This is in reference to the black bark of the tree, which sets it apart from other species of pine trees. Due to it's bark color, the Austrian Pine is also known in various places as the Black Pine or European Black Pine.

English Yew (Taxus Baccata)

Unlike the pines in Harvard Yard, the English Yew has short, glossy needles. The needles of the English Yew can grow to be up to 1 inch long and are very soft to the touch. They are a deep green, waxy color. Similar to the Austrian Pine, the needles are aranged in spirals around the branch and two needles are grown per bud.

The bark of the English Yew is extremely flaky. The bark of the English Yew is a reddith brown color and as the tree ages, the bark becomes more flaky. When the bark peels off of the tree, it reveals the bright orange wood underneath the bark. This cycle then repeats itself when new bark begins to grow at the places where the bark flaked off.

Although the English Yew is poisonous, it has multiple medicinal uses. The English Yew is known for it's beautiful red berries, but these berries are extremely poisonous for humans. Despite the danger that these berries pose, people still use yew trees to make medicines that treat a large array of illnesses. The tips of yew branches as well as their buds are used to treat illnesses including tapeworms, diphtheria, and Urinary Tract Infections.

Cedar of Lebanon (Cedrus Libani)

Cedar of Lebanon needles are bright green and quite fragrant. Cedar of Lebanon has small needles, growing up to 1 inch long. An interesting fact about these needles is the fact that they are not tubular, instead they actually have four sides, creating a very thin square pyramid. The needles grow out of their buds in a circle around the branch in groups of between 20 to 40.

Cedar of Lebanon bark is dark and furrowed. The bark on Cedar of Lebanon trees is a dark grey to black color. As the tree ages, the bark develops deep furrows. This contributes to a scaly bark texture for the Cedar of Lebanon. The scaly bark chip off in small flakes over time, but the wood underneath the first layer is also a dark grey color.

Cedar of Lebanon is a very religiously significant tree in many cultures. Cedar of Lebanon is mentioned in many middle eastern religions, including Christianity and Islam. In Christianity, Cedar of Lebanon is used as a symbol of Jesus' mother. Because of this tree's religious significance, it is also known as Cedar of God in the middle east.

Dawn Redwood (Metasequoia Glyptostroboides)

The Dawn Redwood has needles that are arranged in a leaf-like pattern. The needles of the Dawn Redwood are small individually, growing to only about 1 inch in length. The way that the needles grow is in a perfectly symmetrical fashion, with equal needles growing on either side of the branch that holds them. These needles then create a leaf shape which creates an illusion where if you look at a dawn redwood from a distance, you can't tell that it has needles. These needles aren't like any ordinary needles also! Unlike most conifers, the Dawn Redwood needles actually change color to a beautiful red and drop in the fall, making it one of only a handful of deciduous conifers!

The bark of the Dawn Redwood is red and flaky. As the name suggests, the bark of the dawn Redwood is red! But, in addition to being red, the bark of this tree is also flaky. When the bark flakes off, the wood underneath it is also a red color and is extremely smooth. This then creates a cycle of flaking bark off and regrowing that bark back.

The Dawn Redwood was thought to be extinct for a long time until it was rediscovered! The Dawn Redwood was a tree that scientists had found in the fossil record in China that was thought to be extinct for close to 5 million years. That was until a Chinese forester found one in a forest in the Sichuan Province of China. Another fun fact: The forester who found the thought-to-be-endangered Dawn Redwood had worked at Harvard's very own Arnold Arboretum, and do you know the very first place in the western world that Dawn Redwood seeds were sent? You guessed it, it was Harvard! This history is so special that Harvard's Arnold Arboretum actually uses the Dawn Redwood as it's symbol.

Western Larch (Larix Occidentalis)

The Western Larch has distinct needles that become even more distinct in the fall! The needles of a Western Larch grow up to 2 inches long and grow in clumps of between 15 to 30. In each clump, the needles form a cone shape facing away from the branch. In the fall, the needles of the Western Larch turn a bright yellow color, as seen in the image beneath the name. The Western Larch, like the Dawn Redwood, is one of only a handful of trees that are deciduous conifers where the needles change color and fall in autumn.

The bark of the Western Larch is scaly and flaky. The bark of the Western Larch is deeply fissured and flaky as the tree gets old. When the bark flakes off, it reveals the deep orange wood underneath. The bark of the tree is also very thick, which makes the tree very resistant to fire.

The Western Larch serves as an important host for all kinds of wildlife. The seeds of the Western Larch are a prized food source for many animals such as woodpeckers and other birds. Western Larches when they are also fully grown have been found to be nesting places for Bald Eagles and other large predatory birds like hawks and eagles.
